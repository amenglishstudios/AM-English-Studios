Thinking in Pictures
Temple Grandin is an expert in animal behavior. She also has autism. Some people view autism as a very limiting disability, but Grandin disagrees. She believes that if there were no autism in the world, we would “still be socializing in front of a wood fire at the entrance to a cave ” Why? Grandin thinks that some people with autism have brains that can solve problems “normal” brains cannot.
No one knows for sure what causes autism, but scientists believe it begins during the very early stages of brain development. Autism exists on a spectrum1—from very severe to moderate—and it affects people in different ways. Often, people with autism have trouble socializing and communicating normally with other people. For Grandin, autism also affects the way she thinks and understands the world.
VISUAL THINKER
Grandin, like many people with autism, is a visual thinker—“I think in pictures, not in language,” she says. For example, if you ask most people to think of a shoe, they might “see” in their mind one or two shoes. Grandin, however, remembers all the specific shoes that she knows and visualizes them in her mind. She says that her mind “works like Google Images.” Like Google, she can very quickly categorize the images and arrange them in a specific order. She can also “animate” the images and make them into little movies.
This ability to think visually has helped Grandin in her career. For example, she has a unique understanding of how cattle think. According to animal researchers, cattle think in pictures, not language—similar to how Grandin’s mind works. With this insight, she was able to design plans for livestock farms that reduce the fear that cattle may experience. For example, cattle are easily distracted2 and worried by things like waving flags or even a hose on the ground. Grandin’s designs take away those visual distractions, resulting in calmer livestock.
Grandin’s visual thinking means she can also picture how her designs might work. She creates a mental simulation of her designs and does a test run of the equipment in her mind. She says that the “normal brain ignores the details,” but her brain looks at every tiny aspect of the design. She makes sure the system works even before it’s built.
According to Grandin, an autistic mind is “a specialist mind—good at one thing, bad at something else.” Her autism has made her an expert in animal welfare. She believes that other people with autism may be able to solve global problems or invent some incredible software. As she points out, “the world needs all kinds of minds.”
1spectrum: n. a range of qualities, situations, etc.
2distracted: adj. turn your attention away from something you want to concentrate on

The Genius of a Disordered Mind
What do Mozart, Michelangelo, and Albert Einstein have in common?  Besides being world-renowned geniuses, they also may have had some form of autism.  Although this neurological disorder was not officially diagnosed until the 1900s, researchers are guessing that some of the greatest minds in history may have been autistic.
Autism is a neurological disorder in which nerve cells and synapses in the brain do not connect in the same way as “normal” brains.  Autistic people share the following characteristics:  1) They usually struggle socially; for example, they may have difficulty making friends.  2) Some have noticeable tics or perform some other repetitive action.  3) Most possess strong visual skills and are able to describe something they have seen in incredible detail. 
According to Webster’s dictionary, genius refers to a person whose talent or intelligence is extremely rare.  Researchers wonder if perhaps the historic geniuses listed below could have been diagnosed as autistic today.
·      Michelangelo de Lodovico Buonarroti Simoni’s (1475–1564) artistic and mathematic genius led to some of the most beautiful paintings, sculptures, and buildings in history.  Documents describe him as lacking in social skills and being completely preoccupied with his masterpieces.
·      Wolfgang Amadeus Mozart (1756–1791) was one of the greatest composers and musicians of all time.  It is said that he had to move his hands and feet constantly and was very moody.  He was also described as being obsessed with his thoughts.
·      Albert Einstein (1879–1955) made history through his work in physics.  He was reportedly a lonely child who had difficulty making friends and had an extraordinary curiosity about science.
These men’s social problems and extreme focus on their work mark them as possibly autistic.  The unusual functioning of a genius’ brain has resulted in some of the most amazing art, music, and scientific work the world has ever seen.

Sue Austin—Deep Sea Diving . . . In a Wheelchair

. . I started using a wheelchair 16 years ago when an extended illness changed the way I could access the world. When I started using the wheelchair, it was a tremendous new freedom. I’d seen my life slip away and become restricted. It was like having an enormous new toy. I could whiz around and feel the wind in my face again. Just being out on the street was exhilarating. But even though I had this newfound joy and freedom, people’s reaction completely changed towards me. It was as if they couldn’t see me anymore, as if an invisibility cloak had descended. They seemed to see me in terms of their assumptions of what it must be like to be in a wheelchair. When I asked people their associations with the wheelchair, they used words like limitation, fear, pity, and restriction. I realized I’d internalized these responses and it had changed who I was on a core level. A part of me had become alienated from myself. I was seeing myself not from my perspective, but vividly and continuously from the perspective of other people’s responses to me.
As a result, I knew I needed to make my own stories about this experience, new narratives to reclaim my identity.
