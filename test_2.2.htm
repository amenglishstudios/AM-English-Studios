Messages from the Sea

In the warm, shallow waters of coral reefs live animals with an amazing ability. These animals, known as cephalopods, are able to change the appearance of their skin. Squid, octopi, and cuttlefish are all types of cephalopods. Each of these animals has special cells under its skin that contain pigment, a colored liquid. A cephalopod can move these cells toward or away from its skin. This allows it to change the pattern and color of its appearance.

Cephalopods mainly use these cells as camouflage to hide from predators. For example, cephalopods can blend in to the color of the sea floor, making them invisible to predators. They can also change color to scare predators away. For example, the skin of the Caribbean reef squid is usually a plain brown. When a predator passes close by, the squid makes large “eye” patterns on its skin. This makes the squid appear much bigger than it really is. Squid can also use these cells to communicate other messages. When two male reef squid fight, black and white lines appear on their skin. These send a message that the squid is fierce. A reef squid searching for a mate can flash colorful patterns, sending a message that it is attractive.

Amazingly, reef squid can send two messages at the same time. They can send one message on their right side and a different message on their left side. For example, when males are searching for a mate, they flash attractive patterns on one side of their body. This is the side a female sees. On the other side of their bodies, they can show their fierce zebra stripes. This is the side other males see. They can quickly flip the messages if the female swims to the other side!

With the ability to display 30 colors and patterns, the incredible Caribbean reef squid really is a master of disguise and a skillful communicator.

Artificial Reefs
People create reefs for three main reasons. The first is to protect the coastline. Reefs serve as barriers1 between the coast and powerful ocean storms. Reefs also protect coastlines from erosion2. The Maldives, an island nation in the Indian Ocean, has built reefs to protect its coast from storms that may lead to beach erosion.

The second reason people construct reefs is to promote sea life for recreation and aquaculture. A reef contains many forms of life. Plants, plankton, algae, sponges, eels, fish, crabs, and sea turtles are just some of the organisms that live in healthy reefs. The wide variety of fish makes recreational fishing3 popular around reefs.  Brightly colored fish also make reefs popular with divers and snorkelers. Artificial reefs off the Atlantic coast of the U.S. states of Florida, Georgia, and South Carolina have helped bring more forms of sea life to the area, and this has encouraged tourism.

Aquaculture—also called fish farming--is the cultivation of ocean life for food and industry. A reef provides shelter for many different kinds of fish, so fish farmers can increase their catch by building reefs. Japan creates artificial reefs to encourage the growth of schools4 of snapper and other types of food fish, for instance. Japan is also a leader in creating artificial reefs for pearl-producing oysters. 

A third reason for building reefs is to create waves that promote the sport of surfing. Surfers ride boards on top of waves. Artificial reefs can help make bigger waves and make surfing more fun. El Segundo, California, was the first area to try an artificial reef to improve surfing. Australian beaches at Perth and the Gold Coast have also built artificial reefs for this purpose.

Building underwater barriers is not new. For many years, people who live near the coast have depended on artificial reefs for protection. Two thousand years ago, the Greek historian Strabo wrote that the Persians built reef barriers across the Tigris River to prevent pirates from India from crossing it. 

Ancient peoples like the Persians built artificial reefs out of organic material such as trees and inorganic material such as rocks. These days, engineers use a much wider—and stranger—variety of materials for reef-building.

Sunken ships have provided reef structures for hundreds of years. Today, the engineers who create these artificial reefs do not just sink them. They first remove all plastic and toxic materials from the ships. They then carefully place explosives to sink structures in just the right location. The old aircraft carrier5 USS Oriskany is now a reef in the Gulf of Mexico. New York City subway cars have been sunk for reefs along the East Coast of the U.S. from New Jersey to Georgia.

Reef balls are another type of artificial reef. Reef balls are circular structures with holes for fish and other creatures to swim through and live in. They are constructed mainly of concrete. Reef balls can be used to build a new artificial reef or to help repair a damaged reef. They have been used in Prince William Sound, Alaska, and many other locations.

   wind, moving water, etc.
3 recreational fishing: fishing for fun or for sport, not as a professional activity
4 schools of fish: large groups of fish
5 aircraft carrier: n. a ship that carries military airplanes

Bioluminescence1: Living Light

Animals that use sight to navigate2 generally have a hard time getting around without light. Some, like owls, have very large eyes that they use to collect lots of light. They also use their other senses to gather information about their surroundings. Humans, on the other hand, have put a lot of effort into lighting their way by creating portable artificial light sources, from torches3 to automobile headlights. Bioluminescent life forms have an entirely different approach—they make light themselves and carry it around in their bodies.

You can find bioluminescent life forms all over the world. Some are land animals, such as insects, centipedes, and worms. One of the most widely-known luminescent creatures is the firefly4. People who live near fireflies often think of them as brightly flashing adult insects, but firefly larvae5 glow as well. 

Most of the world's bioluminescence exists in the ocean, not on land, and most live in one particular depth—called the twilight zone6, or the disphotic, zone. It's deeper than the sunlit, or euphotic, zone, but shallower than the midnight, or aphotic zone. The twilight zone extends from about 200 meters to about 1000 meters deep.

Only a small amount of light from the sun reaches the twilight zone. The light that does penetrate this far is bluish-green in color. This is partly because blue-green light has a short wavelength, so it has more energy with which to penetrate the water. Lots of bioluminescent animals live here, including jellyfish, squid, shrimp, marine worms, and fish. Most make light that matches the blue-green sunlight that exists in this part of the ocean. The glow produced by these animals can travel a long way, and it can blend in with the light from above. In some parts of the ocean, these animals, not the sun, are the primary source of light.

1 bioluminescence: n. light that is made by living creatures
2 navigate: v. to move from place to place
3 torches: n. lights carried in the hand
4 firefly: n. a flying insect that emits a flashing light
5 larvae: n. the immature forms of some insects
6 twilight: n. a term usually used to mean the time between day and night. In this passage, “twilight zone” means the part of the ocean between the shallow, sunlit part of the ocean and the deep, dark part of the ocean.

David Gallo—Underwater Astonishments

. . . That’s the unknown world, and today we’ve only explored about three percent of what’s out there in the ocean. Already we’ve found the world’s highest mountains, the world’s deepest valleys, underwater lakes, underwater waterfalls—a lot of that we shared with you from the stage. And in a place where we thought no life at all, we find more life, we think, and diversity and density than the tropical rain forest, which tells us that we don’t know much about this planet at all. There’s still 97 percent, and either that 97 percent is empty or just full of surprises.

But I want to jump up to shallow water now and look at some creatures that are positively amazing. Cephalopods—headfoots. As a kid, I knew them as calamari, mostly. This is an octopus—this is the work of Dr. Roger Hanlon at the Marine Biological Lab—and it’s just fascinating how cephalopods can, with their incredible eyes, sense their surroundings, look at light, look at patterns. Here’s an octopus moving across the reef, finds a spot to settle down, curls up, and then disappears into the background. Tough thing to do.

. . . Cuttlefish. I love cuttlefish. This is a giant Australian cuttlefish. And there he is, his droopy little eyes up here. But they can do pretty amazing things, too. Here we’re going to see one backing into a crevice, and watch his tentacles—he just pulls them in, makes them look just like algae. Disappears right into the background. Positively amazing. Here’s two males fighting. Once again, they’re smart enough, these cephalopods; they know not to hurt each other. But look at the patterns that they can do with their skin. That’s an amazing thing.

Here’s an octopus. Sometimes they don’t want to be seen when they move because predators can see them. Here, this guy actually can make himself look like a rock, and, looking at his environment, can actually slide across the bottom, using the waves and the shadows so he can’t be seen . . . The shallow water’s full of predators—here’s a baracuda—and if you’re an octopus or a cephalopod, you need to really understand how to use your surroundings to hide.
