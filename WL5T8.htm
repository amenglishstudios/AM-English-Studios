     I used to think that dogs were better than cats as pets. As I was growing up, my family had a lot of dogs—mutts, poodles, Chihuahuas, dachshunds—but we never had cats because my father is allergic to (1. them / it / us). My life as a pet owner changed one night when a tiny kitten showed up at my door. He was cold, wet, and hungry and I was afraid he would die on my doorstep if I did not help him. I never saw myself as a cat owner, but now that I am one, I (2. realized / realize / had realized) that most of the stereotypes about cats and dogs are untrue.
     Cats are supposed to be standoffish and (3. disinteresting / disinterested / disinterest) as well as finicky and meticulous. My cat, however, is very friendly and even a little messy. He always (4. wants / want / wanted) my attention, which can be annoying at times, especially when I am trying to cook or study.
     People also say that dogs are (5. more friendly / more friendlier / friendlier) than cats, but I remember a couple of my family's dogs that were not friendly at all. Our Chihuahua was named Wagster, and he did not like anyone. Once he even nipped at my grandmother! We also had another dog named Rover that used to bark loudly whenever anyone came to our house. Even though our mail carrier never met Rover, I am certain (6. he / it / they) knew that Rover was not a friendly dog.
     Of course, I have also known both cats and dogs that fulfill the stereotypes. My friend Aimee has a cat that is so aloof that I never see it when I (7. visit / visited / have visited). Whenever I visit, Bono jumps all over me.
     How did these stereotypes about cats and dogs develop? As with most prejudices, (9. I / we / they) reflect more about the people who express them than about what is true. Both cats and dogs make good pets, and both of them can make bad pets. It all depends on the individual animal, and no one (10. could / should / has to) judge an animal before getting to know it.
