Art in the Community

On a street in New Orleans is an old building. It used to be a grocery store. But now it is vacant. On the boards covering the windows are stickers. Each sticker says “I wish this was . . . ” Someone has written “full of food” on one of the stickers.

When we think of public art, we often think of sculptures or other large works of art in city centers. Candy Chang’s art, however, is somewhere else. Chang makes her art in a city’s older or poorer neighborhoods. She uses the outside walls of vacant buildings as her canvas. She paints, writes messages, or puts stickers on them. Chang wants to reach out to people with her art. She sees it as the start of a conversation—a conversation with local residents.

In 2010, Chang started the “I Wish This Was” project in New Orleans. Chang asked residents to look at the abandoned buildings in their community. She wanted them to think about what else could be there. She printed hundreds of small stickers and stuck them on these buildings. The stickers said, “I wish this was” in large letters and included a space for people to write. Anyone could write anything. Chang was amazed at the reaction. Hundreds of residents wrote on the stickers. Someone wrote, “I wish this was a grocery store.” Another person wrote, “I wish this was Heaven.”

There are many abandoned buildings in neighborhoods around the world. It’s hard for residents to create a close neighborhood if the streets are full of empty buildings. By making art that gives residents a voice, Chang hopes to build stronger communities. Chang’s art projects get residents thinking and talking about problems in their community. This initiative may help city planners to create better cities—ones that truly meet the needs of residents.

The Recycled Orchestra

Paragraph A
Not far from Asunción, the capital of Paraguay, is the village of Cateura. It sits on top of a massive landfill1 that receives 1,500 tons of new trash each day. The village is home to several thousand families who make a living by sorting through the waste, separating out whatever is useful or whatever can be sold to the local recycling industry. According to UNICEF2, Cateura is a community with extreme poverty, illiteracy, and pollution. Amazingly, it’s also home to an orchestra—one made up of local children whose instruments are made entirely from recycled trash.

Paragraph B
“Los Reciclados,” or The Recycled Orchestra, was formed several years ago when environmentalist and musician Favio Chávez decided to open a music school. He partnered with a local garbage picker Nicolás Gómez—a genius at building useful things from trash. There was no money for real instruments, so together they made instruments from the trash around them. Oil drums became violins and cellos, water pipes and bottle caps turned into flutes, and packing crates grew into guitars. The fact that the instruments are made from recycled trash has a practical purpose as well: The kids can safely carry them. For many children, it was impossible to give them a violin to take home because they had nowhere to keep it and their parents were afraid they would be robbed. A real violin would be worth more than a house in Cateura.  

Paragraph C
Chávez started training just a few children, but today the orchestra is made up of 35 kids from Cateura. Chávez gives music lessons to another 200, who also learn how to build their own recycled instruments. Cateura may seem like the unlikeliest of homes for a classical music community. But for the children who’ve come to love it, music has given them a greater purpose. These ingenious musicians have managed to transform their reality into beautiful symphonies by Mozart and Beethoven. As one young musician says, “My life would be worthless without music.”

Paragraph D
The Recycled Orchestra has become the focus of international attention over the last few years. It is the subject of a documentary film about the kids, Landfill Harmonic, which has gone viral. The orchestra has gone on a tour of several South American countries, with plans to tour soon in the U.S. and Europe. Meanwhile, Chávez will continue his work in Cateura. "Sports can be competitive. Music causes children to connect and feel they are building something together. Our orchestra feels special because the children make beauty out of garbage."

1 landfill: n. a place where garbage is buried in the ground
2 UNICEF: United Nations Children’s Fund

How to Turn Anything into Something Else

Philadelphia is well-known for its public art. There are some famous sculptures, such as the LOVE sculpture in JFK Plaza, where tourists often stand to have their photos taken. There are also well-known murals1. One of the most interesting of these is called “How to Turn Anything into Something Else.” It was created by 31 students, ages 10–15. The students worked with a group of artists from a nationally known art collective. They helped the students access their own imaginations.

The students took a piece of paper and drew the head of an animal at the top. When they had finished drawing it, they folded the paper so that only the neck of their drawing was visible. Then it was passed to the next student, who drew a torso2 of an animal. The folding and passing was repeated until an entire creature was drawn on the paper. This exercise was designed to show students that art does not have to end the way it begins. The students created hundreds of images over the summer. They were directly included in the mural.

1 mural: n. a large painting that is painted directly on a wall
2 torso: n. the part of the body between the neck and the legs

DIRECTIONS: Read the excerpt from Candy Chang’s 2012 TED Talk and answer the questions.

CANDY CHANG—Before I Die, I Want to . . .

Paragraph 1

There are a lot of ways the people around us can help improve our lives. We don’t bump into every neighbor, so a lot of wisdom never gets passed on, though we do share the same public spaces. . . .

Paragraph 2

I live near this house, and I thought about how I could make it a nicer space for my neighborhood, and I also thought about something that changed my life forever.

Paragraph 3

In 2009, I lost someone I loved very much. Her name was Joan, and she was a mother to me, and her death was sudden and unexpected. And I thought about death a lot, and this made me feel deep gratitude for the time I’ve had, and brought clarity to the things that are meaningful to my life now. But I struggle to maintain this perspective in my daily life. I feel like it’s easy to get caught up in the day-to-day, and forget what really matters to you.

Paragraph 4

So, with help from old and new friends, I turned the side of this abandoned house into a giant chalkboard and stenciled it with a fill-in-the-blank sentence: “Before I die, I want to ...”  So anyone walking by can pick up a piece of chalk, reflect on their lives, and share their personal aspirations in public space.

Paragraph 5

I didn’t know what to expect from this experiment, but by the next day, the wall was entirely filled out, and it kept growing...
