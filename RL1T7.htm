GOING VIRAL

In 1968, the artist Andy Warhol predicted, “In the future, everybody will be world-famous for 15 minutes.” Today, it seems his prediction is becoming true.

The tune is simple, and the lyrics don’t make sense. But in 2011, 13-year-old Rebecca Black’s song “Friday” became a sensation. Black recorded the song as a music video for a small record company. The company posted it on YouTube. In its first month online, very few people saw it. Then a popular blogger wrote about the video. He called “Friday” the worst video ever made.

200 MILLION VIEWS
Suddenly, the video went viral. Everyone wanted to see if “Friday” was really so bad. People often watched the video on Friday. Soon, other people posted similar music videos, inspired by “Friday.” For example, a famous comedian posted a video called “Thursday.” It had the same tune but different words. Its lyrics didn’t make sense either, but they were very funny. By the end of 2011, “Friday” had over 200 million views. It was one of the most-watched videos on YouTube. Black was famous.

INSTANT FAME
In the 21st century, our definition of fame has changed. Online video-sharing sites like YouTube make it possible for ordinary people—instead of movie companies or magazines, for example—to decide who is famous. We all now have more ownership of our own pop culture, and no-one has to green-light our ideas—you can upload your own videos or publish your own work at any time. However, this fame may not be based on any actual talent. You could be famous for being a very bad singer or just doing something ridiculous. For some people, this can mean that fame happens when we don’t expect it or even when we don’t want it.

Realistically, though, your chances of becoming famous are still fairly small. In fact, 90 percent of videos viewed on YouTube are of people, like pop stars, who are already famous.

The Bicycle Fad

Paragraph 1

A fad is a sudden, enthusiastic interest in a product, a fashion, or an idea. A fad catches on when the number of people following it increases quickly. After a certain amount of time, however—it can be weeks, months, or years—interest becomes less. It may not completely disappear, but it becomes far less intense. In the Internet Age, people are so well-connected that a fad—such as the interest in viral videos—can appear quickly and then disappear in the same amount of time. There were fads, however, long before the Internet existed.

Paragraph 2

One example was the bicycle fad of the late 1800s. Early forms of the bicycle—called the velocipede—had appeared in the early part of the 19th century, but they were not very practical. One type of velocipede, introduced in France in 1865, was so uncomfortable to ride that it was called “the boneshaker.” The high-wheelers (called “penny-farthings” in the U.K.) of the early 1880s came next. These had one large wheel in front—up to 64 inches (163 centimeters) in diameter—and a much smaller one in back. To be able to ride these not only took a lot of skill, but also cost a lot of money. A typical high-wheeler cost about the same as a six months’ salary for the average worker of the time. You also needed to be very brave, as accidents were common. The so-called “safety bicycle,” first manufactured in Coventry, U.K. in 1885, looked much more like the bicycles we are familiar with today, and was the first machine to be called a “bicycle.”

Paragraph 3

By the late 1880s, bicycling had become a huge fad in North America and Europe, and —unlike many fads—it had a lot of consequences. By 1890, there were around 300 bicycle factories just in the U.S., and in that year, they made more than a million bicycles. Bicycles were used not only for basic transportation but also for leisure. The late 1800s was a time when the middle class started to have more free time. Bicycle races became popular. Bicycle clubs were formed and huge numbers of people would go for rides in the country on weekends. Bicyclists demanded that national and local governments improve roads. Both men and women participated in the fad. As bicycles became safer and cheaper, more women had access to the personal freedom that bicycles gave them, and so the bicycle became a symbol of the “New Woman “of the late nineteenth century, especially in the U.K. and the U.S. It even changed women’s clothing. The long dresses women had worn prior to the fad were not suitable for bicycling, and “bicycle suits” became a common form of dress.

Paragraph 4

By about 1910, interest in bicycles began to decrease, as interest in cars and motorcycles grew. For the next 60 years, in the U.S. and Europe, bicycles were mainly used by children who were too young to drive. In the 1970s, bicycles were rediscovered. Adults began to use them as a cheap, environmentally friendly alternative to cars. New types of bicycles—such as mountain bikes—were used for recreation. This was the start of a new bicycle fad that continues to this very day.

Gangnam Style

While popular in his own country, Psy was not well-known in the rest of the world. But a week after releasing the video for the song, a famous rapper tweeted about how great the video was. Suddenly, many other famous people posted messages about the video. Millions of people read the messages and went to view the video on YouTube. Within days, “Gangnam Style” went viral. Today, the video has been watched over 2 billion times.

DIRECTIONS: Read the excerpt from Kevin Allocca’s 2011 TED Talk and answer the questions.

Kevin Allocca—Why Videos Go Viral

Hi. I’m Kevin Allocca. I’m the trends manager at YouTube, and I professionally watch YouTube videos. It’s true. So we’re going to talk a little bit today about how videos go viral and then why that even matters. We all want to be stars— celebrities, singers, comedians—and when I was younger, that seemed so very, very hard to do. But now Web video has made it so that any of us or any of the creative things that we do can become completely famous in a part of our world’s culture. Any one of you could be famous on the Internet by next Saturday. But there are over 48 hours of video uploaded to YouTube every minute. And of that, only a tiny percentage ever goes viral and gets tons of views and becomes a cultural moment. So how does it happen? Three things: tastemakers, communities of participation, and unexpectedness. ...

... Last year, Bear Vasquez posted this video that he had shot outside his home in Yosemite National Park. In 2010, it was viewed 23 million times. This is a chart of what it looked like when it first became popular last summer. But he didn’t actually set out to make a viral video, Bear. He just wanted to share a rainbow. Because that's what you do when your name is Yosemite Mountain Bear. And he had posted lots of nature videos in fact. . . .

And this video had actually been posted all the way back in January. So what happened here? Jimmy Kimmel, actually. Jimmy Kimmel posted this tweet that would eventually propel the video to be as popular as it would become. Because tastemakers like Jimmy Kimmel introduce us to new and interesting things and bring them to a larger audience.
