"Development of the Internet"

     Did you know that the Internet began as a defense department project? During the 1960's, the United States and the Soviet Union were in a Cold War. Both countries had nuclear weapons. The United States wanted a safe communication system if it was attacked. Telephones and radio communications had central controls and could fail easily. The search for a more safe communication system soon evolved into what we today call the "internet."

     The first step in this process began in 1969. The Defense Department's Advanced Research Project Agency asked the Rand Institute to develop a more secure communication system. The Rand Institute developed a system that connected four university supercomputers. It had no central control. If one part of the system failed, another network could take over. Messages were successfully sent back and forth between scientists at these universities.

     Next, in the 1970's, many advances were made to this original system. New computer languages were developed and files could be transferred. The Intel Company invented the first microcomputer chip. Then in 1973, the first International Internet connections were established to England and Norway.

     After these technical successes, personal computers became more available. As personal computers became more popular, the Internet users increased. In 1989, there were 80,000 Internet users, but in 1994, there were over 2.2 million users. In 1995, the Internet became more commercial and businesses and advertising became the main support of the Internet.

     Today the amount of Internet users has expanded worldwide. People now can shop, study, and do research all from their own home. What once began as a defense project has now become a global communication network.
