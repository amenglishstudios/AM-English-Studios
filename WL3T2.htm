        Pollution often does not stay in the area where it first enters the ecosystem. (1) First, weather patterns can move air pollutants over long distances. This can easily be seen in Michigan. There, the wind blows air pollution produced in the industrial south to the rural north—a place that should have little pollution but which, in reality, is highly contaminated. (2) For instance, pollutants dumped in waterways can be carried downstream. Fertilizer washed into the Mississippi River in Memphis, Tennessee, contaminates water that passes through New Orleans, Louisiana. (3) Finally, pollutants may be relocated by humans themselves. (4) On the other hand, large container ships use water, rocks, and soil from their home ports as ballast. These items contain local pollutants. (5) When the ship reaches its destination, it will dump its polluted ballast, in contrast contaminating a new area. (6) For example, pollution is a serious problem that cannot be tackled without first understanding its complexity.
