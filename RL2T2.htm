Trouble for the Air Plants
In the rain forests of Costa Rica, scientist Nalini Nadkarni climbs a giant strangler fig tree to reach the canopy layer. The canopy, she says, is “like the atmosphere of an open field.” Home to thousands of unique plant species, rain forests are important to the health of our planet. But the canopy layer is in trouble.
THE MYSTERIOUS CANOPY
As a forest ecologist, Nadkarni uses mountain-climbing techniques, construction cranes, and hot-air balloons to research life in the mysterious canopy. High up in the canopy layer, she studies a special group of plants called epiphytes. Worldwide, there are over 28,000 species of epiphytes, and mosses are the most common type. Epiphytes are sometimes called “air plants” because most do not have roots in the ground. Also, many of them live more than 100 feet (over 30 meters) above the forest floor. So how do these unusual plants survive?
Epiphyte roots attach to the branches of other plants, particularly trees. However, epiphytes don’t take nutrients from their host tree. Instead, they absorb water and nutrients from the atmosphere. Nadkarni says that epiphytes have “a tremendous capacity for holding on to nutrients and water.” Some host trees take some of these nutrients, too, through tiny roots that grow out of their branches. Because they grow so high in the canopy, epiphytes are also able to get energy from the sun.
CLINGING TO LIFE
These canopy communities of epiphytes are fragile1; epiphytes need a specific balance of light, nutrients, and water to survive. In recent years, humans have destroyed many rain forests to make room for agriculture and other activities. This has changed the balance of light, nutrients2, and water in the environment. Nadkarni says that epiphytes are an essential part of the rain forest, and not just “insignificant bits of green.” Epiphytes play a vital part in keeping the climate stable. They keep the rain forests cooler and wetter by storing and then releasing huge amounts of water.
In the temperate rain forests of the Pacific Northwest, epiphytes are facing problems of a different kind. There, mosses are stripped3 from the trees and sold to florists and garden centers. It’s a big industry that supplies jobs and income for the region. Unfortunately, these mosses take a long time—over 20 years—to regenerate. Once they are damaged, mosses cannot easily grow back. Farming epiphytes is not sustainable, so Nadkarni wondered, “What can I, as an ecologist, do about that?”
RECONNECTING PEOPLE
Nadkarni realized that it wasn’t enough to tell other scientists about the problems in the rain forests. She needed to reach out to the general public. Over the past few years, she has developed innovative projects to “reconnect people with trees.” She has worked with rappers, writers, and artists to capture the attention of young people in particular. Through these projects, Nadkarni is spreading the word about the importance of epiphytes and the world’s forest canopies.

1fragile: adj. easily damaged or broken
2nutrients: n. ingredients that plants and animals need in order to live
3strip: v. to take off or remove

A Part of the Solution
Tree researcher Nalini Nadkarni has spent a lot of time up in trees.  Lately though, she’s been spending a lot of time on the ground.  She’s been working more with people than with trees.  Her goal has been to make more and more people aware of environmental problems.  She strongly believes that the huge environmental problems—such as global climate change, extinction of animal and plant species, and forest fragmentation (cutting down too many trees so that only a small part of the forest is left)—cannot be solved by scientists alone.  Everyone needs to be involved.
Nadkarni knows that all people care about the environment to some degree.  They just may not know about the problems going on or the possible negative consequences.  So Nadkarni and her team have developed different ways to educate people on environmental issues.  Three of these ways have been recreational, aesthetic, and spiritual.
•      Recreational: Nadkarni and her team created a “tree-top Barbie”—a doll dressed up with tree-climbing gear.  They hope children will play with the doll and pretend to be tree researchers—becoming more familiar with the profession.
•      Aesthetic: All people have a sense of what is beautiful.  Many people express this through fashion.  Nadkarni’s team has come up with a sports jacket with a nature print on it.  The jacket can be a conversation starter about the beauty of nature and how to protect it.
•      Spiritual: Nadkarni found that in all major religions, holy books make many references to trees.  She collected these references and met with religious groups.  She told them how important trees are according to their own scriptures. 
By targeting different groups of people, such as children, fashion-conscious people, and religious people, Nadkarni is helping more people become aware of the environment.  And once people are aware of certain environmental problems, Nadkarni hopes, they will become a part of the solution.
