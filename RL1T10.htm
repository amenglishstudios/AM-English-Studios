ROBOTS LIKE US

Meet Nexi. Nexi is one of a new generation of robots. It moves on wheels and can pick up objects. But robots like Nexi are more than just entertaining toys.

INTERACTING WITH ROBOTS

Roboticists at the Massachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) call Nexi a “social robot.” A social robot is a robot that can communicate like a human. It can express human emotions. Nexi can look sad, mad, and even bored. Some social robots can also “read” our facial expressions. For example, Nexi can see that we are sad and can say something to make us happy. Roboticists, like MIT’s Cynthia Breazeal, believe that it won’t be long before we have social robots in our homes, helping us live better lives.

Autom is another social robot developed at MIT. Autom is a robot health coach. It has a screen where you enter information about what food you ate that day or how much you exercised. Autom has a face with eyes that move. It speaks with a synthesized voice. Autom gives you advice about your diet and exercise, and motivates you to do better.

In a test, Autom helped people lose weight better than other ways of recording diet information. Breazeal thinks this is because Autom used facial expressions and words of encouragement. In other words, Autom interacted like a real person. In fact, many people who used Autom gave the robot a new name and talked to it. When robots use human expressions and body language, Breazeal says, people respond to them “a lot like they respond to people.”

In the future, humans may interact with social robots just like they do with other people. The robot of the future will be not just a machine; it may also be your coach, helper, or friend.

Us. And Them.

Paragraph 1

Someone types a command into a laptop, and Actroid-DER sits up quickly. Air flows beneath silicone skin, raising her arms and lifting the corners of her mouth into a smile. She blinks, then turns her face toward me and says, “Are you surprised that I’m a robot?” she asks. “I look just like a human, don’t I?”

Paragraph 2

Unfortunately, her programmed comment makes me realize the many ways that she does not. Developed in Japan by the Kokoro Company, the Actroid-DER android can be rented to serve as a speaker at corporate events. But in spite of the $250,000 spent on making her, she does not move smoothly and her face is inelastic1.

Paragraph 3

This model has been transported to Carnegie Mellon University in Pittsburgh to acquire something like a personality. At least this is what five optimistic graduate students in the university’s Entertainment Technology Center are hoping. They have been given 15 weeks to make this robot seem more like a human female. They have begun by renaming her Yume—dream, in Japanese. “The Kokoro Company developed her to be physically realistic, but that’s not enough by itself,” says Christine Barnes, student co-producer of the Yume Project. “What we’re going to do is shift the focus from realism to believability.”

Paragraph 4

The Actroid androids are part of a new generation of robots designed to function not as programmed machines, but independently—capable of taking on roles in our homes, schools, and offices previously done only by humans. The first examples of this type of robot were the Roomba robots that can vacuum our carpets, and the electronic pets that sit up and roll over on command. More advanced robots may soon be available that cook for us, fold the laundry, even take care of our children or elderly parents, while we watch and assist from a computer miles away.  “In five or ten years, robots will routinely be functioning in human environments,” says Reid Simmons, a professor of robotics at Carnegie Mellon.

Paragraph 5

This leads to many questions. How much everyday human function do we want to give to machines? Do we want robots like Yume cooking in our kitchens? How will the robot revolution change the way we relate to each other? In short: Are we ready for them? Are they ready for us?

Shigeo Hirose: Robot Designer

Professor Shigeo Hirose teaches and does research in the Department of Mechanical and Aerospace Engineering at the Tokyo Institute of Technology. He is renowned for his pioneering work in the design and control of robots. Some of his early projects involved “bio-inspiration.” In other words, he used animals as inspirations for his robot creations.

Hirose actually went to a pet store and purchased a snake in order to study the way it moved. He said that a snake has a simple, slender body, much like a string, but it not only crawls along the ground, it can also coil around an object and leap from branch to branch. He used his studies of a real snake’s locomotion to create a robot that could slither along the ground and wrap itself around objects. He has also created robots that can walk, swim, hop, and skate, as well as a “ninja” robot that can climb vertical walls.

TED Talk Comprehension

DIRECTIONS: Read the excerpt from Cynthia Breazeal’s 2010 TED Talk and answer the questions.

Cynthia Breazeal—The Rise of Personal Robots

Ever since I was a little girl seeing Star Wars for the first time, I’ve been fascinated by this idea of personal robots. And as a little girl, I loved the idea of a robot that interacted with us much more like a helpful, trusted sidekick—something that would delight us, enrich our lives, and help us save a galaxy or two. I knew robots like that didn’t really exist, but I knew I wanted to build them. . . .

So over the past several years I’ve been continuing to explore this interpersonal dimension of robots, now at the Media Lab with my own team of incredibly talented students. And one of my favorite robots is Leonardo. We developed Leonardo in collaboration with Stan Winston Studio. ..

... what I’ve learned through building these systems is that robots are actually a really intriguing social technology, where it’s actually their ability to push our social buttons and to interact with us like a partner that is a core part of their functionality. And with that shift in thinking, we can now start to imagine new questions, new possibilities for robots that we might not have thought about otherwise. But what do I mean when I say “push our social buttons”? Well, one of the things that we’ve learned is that, if we design these robots to communicate with us using the same body language, the same sort of non-verbal cues that people use—like Nexi, our humanoid robot, is doing here—what we find is that people respond to robots a lot like they respond to people. People use these cues to determine things like how persuasive someone is, how likable, how engaging, how trustworthy. It turns out it’s the same for robots. . .
