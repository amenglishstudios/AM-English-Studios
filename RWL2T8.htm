"Concerns about Genetically Modified Foods"

     Did you know that two-thirds of American processed food contain genetically modified ingredients? Transferring a gene from one organism into the DNA of another makes genetically modified crops. In order for this to happen, a "carrier," such as a virus is used to transfer the DNA material. Proponents of this technology say these advances could result in higher food yields, more nutritious foods, and more pesticide resistant crops. However, early evidence suggests that the process of genetically modifying crops can be dangerous to our health and the environment and I believe more research should be done before we use this technology widely.

     One environmental area of concern is the inability to isolate genetically modified crops from other agricultural products. Recently, many corn-based products were found to be contaminated with a popular genetically modified corn feed. Unfortunately, this was an animal feed and was not approved for human consumption. So how did this corn feed get into these other foods? Scientists think that pollen drift and feed residues on farm equipment transferred the ingredient to other corn crops. Because of the contamination, over three hundred corn-based products were recalled.

     This new technology also may affect the health of individuals in unpredictable ways. For example, the corn-feed contamination posed serious health hazards to allergic individuals; in fact, many consumers said they suffered serious allergic reactions from unknowingly eating these contaminated foods. Many of these individuals sought damages from the corn feed company.

     Proponents of genetically modified foods say that labeling foods that are genetically modified could solve health problems. However, at this time, there are no U.S. laws that require labeling for domestically genetically modified products. The Food and Drug Agency has resisted mandatory U.S. labeling due to the costs involved. Instead, it has proposed voluntary labeling for companies that want to alert consumers to the presence or absence of genetically modified ingredients. Many claim that labeling can help prevent these health hazards. They also claim that better technology will prevent pollen spread and contamination.
